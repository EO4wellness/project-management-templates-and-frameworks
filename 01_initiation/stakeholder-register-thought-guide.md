# Stakeholder Register — Thought Guide

This guide helps you think deeply and systematically about stakeholders during project initiation. Use it to avoid blind spots, uncover hidden influencers, and build a realistic engagement strategy before planning begins.

---

# 1. Identifying Stakeholders

### Key Questions
- Who will be impacted by this project’s success or failure?
- Who has authority, influence, or decision-making power?
- Who provides resources, funding, or approvals?
- Who will use the final product or service?
- Who will maintain or support the solution after delivery?
- Who might resist the project or feel threatened by it?
- Are there regulatory, compliance, or legal stakeholders?
- Are there external partners, vendors, or contractors involved?

### Blind Spots to Check
- End users (often forgotten)
- Support teams (IT, HR, Finance, Operations)
- Compliance, legal, or security groups
- Adjacent teams affected indirectly
- Leadership who may not be hands-on but still influential
- Community or public stakeholders (for nonprofits or public-sector work)

---

# 2. Understanding Influence, Interest, and Support

### Influence
- Who can accelerate or block progress?
- Who controls budget, approvals, or resources?
- Who shapes organizational priorities?

### Interest
- Who cares deeply about the outcome?
- Who is impacted day-to-day?
- Who has something to gain or lose?

### Support Level
- Who is naturally supportive?
- Who is neutral?
- Who may resist or challenge the project?

### Prompts
- What motivates this stakeholder?
- What concerns might they have?
- What pressures or constraints do they face?
- What does “success” look like from their perspective?

### Authority
- Who actually has decision‑making authority, not just a senior title?
- Is authority centralized (one sponsor) or distributed (many managers)?
- Are there multiple leaders with overlapping domains?
- Who controls budget? Who controls resources? Who controls approvals?
- Who can block progress even if they aren’t formally “in charge”?
- Are there informal influencers who shape decisions behind the scenes?
- Does the organization favor consensus, hierarchy, or autonomy?

### Risk Patterns to Watch For
- If your project includes many stakeholder managers, unclear authority → delays, conflicting direction
- One sponsor but many “advisors” → hidden veto power
- Matrixed teams → competing priorities and resource conflicts
- Strong functional silos → slow alignment, slow decisions
- High turnover or reorganizations → shifting authority mid‑project

### Blind Spots
- Assuming the org chart reflects real power
- Treating all managers as equal decision‑makers
- Ignoring informal influencers (the person everyone listens to)
- Underestimating how many approvals are needed
- Not identifying who can say “no” even if they can’t say “yes”

---

# 3. Engagement Strategy Thinking

### Questions to Guide Strategy
- What does each stakeholder need to feel informed and confident?
- How often do they need updates?
- What communication style do they prefer?
- Do they need high-level summaries or detailed reports?
- Should communication be synchronous (meetings) or asynchronous (email, dashboards)?
- Who should deliver the communication (PM, sponsor, SME)?

### Engagement Types to Consider
- Inform (keep updated)
- Consult (seek input)
- Involve (collaborate)
- Approve (sign-off)
- Monitor (observe progress)

### Blind Spots
- Over-communicating to low-interest stakeholders
- Under-communicating to high-influence stakeholders
- Assuming one-size-fits-all communication
- Forgetting to adjust strategies as the project evolves


---

# 4. Communication Requirements

### Prompts
- What information does this stakeholder need to do their job?
- What information helps them feel confident in the project?
- What information prevents surprises or escalations?
- What format works best (email, dashboard, meeting, report)?
- What cadence is realistic and sustainable?

### Considerations
- Some stakeholders need only milestone updates.
- Others need weekly or even daily touchpoints.
- Some prefer visuals; others prefer written summaries.
- Some want risks highlighted; others want solutions emphasized.

---

# 5. Stakeholder-Related Risks

### Questions to Uncover Risks
- Who might resist the project or its outcomes?
- Who might feel threatened by changes in process, tools, or roles?
- Who has competing priorities that could delay decisions?
- Who might withdraw support if expectations aren’t managed?
- What happens if a key stakeholder becomes unavailable?

### Risk Patterns to Watch For
- High influence + low support = major risk
- High interest + low influence = potential advocate
- Low interest + high influence = requires careful management

---

# 6. Validating the Register

### Prompts
- Have you reviewed the register with the sponsor?
- Have you validated assumptions with SMEs?
- Are influence and interest ratings consistent across stakeholders?
- Are engagement strategies realistic given team capacity?
- Does the register reflect the *current* state of the project?

### Reminder
Stakeholder Registers are **living documents**.  
Revisit and update them as the project evolves.

---

# 7. Optional: AI Brainstorming (NDA-Safe)

AI tools can help you:
- brainstorm potential stakeholder groups
- draft engagement strategies
- explore communication styles
- identify risks or blind spots

**Only use AI tools that are NDA-safe and never enter confidential, proprietary, or sensitive information.**  
Use AI for ideation — not for storing or processing stakeholder data.

---

# 8. Final Reflection

### Ask Yourself
- Do I understand who truly holds influence?
- Do I understand who will feel the impact most?
- Have I captured motivations, concerns, and expectations?
- Have I built a realistic plan to engage each stakeholder?
- Does this register help prevent surprises later?

A strong Stakeholder Register reduces risk, improves alignment, and sets the foundation for smoother planning and execution.
