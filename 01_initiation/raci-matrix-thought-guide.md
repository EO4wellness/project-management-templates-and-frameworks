# RACI Matrix — Thought Guide

This guide helps you think strategically about roles, responsibilities, and decision-making during project initiation. A well-designed RACI prevents confusion, reduces bottlenecks, and ensures accountability throughout the project lifecycle.

Strong role clarity early prevents future downstream conflict.

---

# 1. Purpose of the RACI

### Key Questions
- What decisions or deliverables require clear ownership?
- Where has responsibility been unclear in similar projects?
- What tasks are high-risk if roles are ambiguous?
- How will this RACI support alignment across teams?

### Blind Spots
- Creating a RACI because “PMs are supposed to” rather than to solve real clarity gaps.
- Over-documenting low-impact tasks.
- Ignoring cross-functional or external roles.

---

# 2. Understanding Roles & Authority Structures

### Prompts
- Who actually has authority — not just on paper, but in practice?
- Who must approve decisions?
- Who can block progress?
- Who has expertise that must be consulted?
- Who needs visibility to feel confident?

### Considerations
- Sponsors often hold A (Accountable) for major decisions.
- SMEs may be C (Consulted) for technical tasks.
- PMs are often R (Responsible) for coordination, not content.
- External partners may require I (Informed) status for alignment.

### Blind Spots
- Assigning A to someone who lacks real authority.
- Forgetting informal influencers.
- Overloading the PM with too many R roles.

---

# 3. Identifying Tasks & Deliverables

### Prompts
- What are the major deliverables in the project?
- What decisions must be made?
- What tasks require cross-functional collaboration?
- What tasks are high-risk or high-visibility?

### Considerations
- Use tasks that reflect the project lifecycle.
- Avoid overly granular tasks — keep it meaningful.
- Include decision points, not just deliverables.

### Blind Spots
- Missing early-phase tasks (initiation, planning).
- Missing late-phase tasks (handoff, closure).
- Forgetting external dependencies or vendor tasks.

---

# 4. Assigning R, A, C, and I

### Prompts
- Who is doing the work? (R)
- Who owns the outcome? (A)
- Who must be consulted before decisions? (C)
- Who needs visibility but not involvement? (I)

### Considerations
- Each task must have exactly one A.
- Multiple Rs are acceptable but should be intentional.
- Cs should be limited to those who add value.
- Is should be included for transparency, not noise.

### Blind Spots
- Too many Cs → slows decisions.
- Too many Rs → unclear ownership.
- Missing Is → stakeholders feel excluded.
- Assigning A to someone who is **not available or engaged**.

---

# 5. Matrixed Teams & Cross-Functional Work

### Prompts
- Are teams distributed across departments or time zones?
- Are SMEs shared across multiple projects?
- Are there external vendors or partners?
- Are responsibilities shifting by phase?

### Considerations
- Document phase-based changes (e.g., SME consulted only during design).
- Clarify vendor responsibilities early.
- Identify where handoffs occur.

### Blind Spots
- Assuming availability of shared resources.
- Not documenting conditional responsibilities.
- Forgetting to align with the Communication Plan.

---

# 6. Aligning the RACI with the Stakeholder Register

### Prompts
- Do RACI roles match stakeholder influence and interest?
- Are high-influence stakeholders appropriately placed as A or C?
- Are low-influence stakeholders placed as I?

### Considerations
- The Stakeholder Register informs who needs visibility.
- The RACI clarifies who does what.
- Both documents must tell the same story.

### Blind Spots
- Conflicting roles between documents.
- Overlooking stakeholders with high informal influence.

---

# 7. Aligning the RACI with the Communication Plan

### Prompts
- Do RACI assignments match communication responsibilities?
- Are escalation paths reflected in A assignments?
- Are Is aligned with communication frequency?

### Considerations
- A roles often align with escalation recipients.
- C roles often align with meeting participants.
- I roles often align with dashboard/report recipients.

### Blind Spots
- Misalignment between RACI and communication channels.
- Assigning A to someone who is not part of the communication loop.

---

# 8. Handling Out-of-Scope Requests in the RACI

### Prompts
- Who is Responsible for logging out-of-scope requests?
- Who is Accountable for approving or rejecting them?
- Who must be Consulted for evaluation?
- Who must be Informed of decisions?

### Considerations
- This should align with the Communication Plan’s out-of-scope pathway.
- Sponsors are often A for scope changes.
- PMs are typically R for logging and coordination.

### Blind Spots
- Not assigning A for scope decisions.
- Forgetting to include SMEs as C for evaluation.

---

# 9. Common RACI Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

### Pitfalls
- Too many As → no clear owner.
- Too many Rs → unclear responsibility.
- Too many Cs → slow decisions.
- Too many Is → communication overload.
- Missing A → task stalls.
- Assigning A to someone without authority.

### Solutions
- Enforce the “one A per task” rule.
- Limit Cs to those who add value.
- Use Is intentionally.
- Validate assignments with stakeholders.

# 10. Optional: AI Brainstorming (NDA-Safe)

AI can help you:
- identify unclear responsibilities
- explore alternative role assignments
- test for bottlenecks
- identify missing stakeholders
- generate draft RACIs for review

**Never include confidential or sensitive information.**  
Use AI for ideation, not storage.

---

# 11. Final Reflection

### Ask Yourself
- Does every task have one clear owner?
- Do assignments reflect real authority, not assumptions?
- Are responsibilities balanced and realistic?
- Does the RACI align with the Stakeholder Register and Communication Plan?
- Have stakeholders validated their roles?
- Does the matrix support clarity, accountability, and smooth decision-making?

Clear roles create confident teams and predictable outcomes.
